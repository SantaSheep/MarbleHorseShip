===Introduction to Purchasing Power===
Purchasing power is a term used to describe the ability of an individual or group to purchase goods and services. It is a measure of the amount of goods and services that can be purchased with a given amount of money. Purchasing power is affected by a variety of factors, including inflation, [[Exchange Rate|exchange rate]]s, and the availability of goods and services. It is an important concept in economics, as it helps to explain how changes in the economy can affect the purchasing power of individuals and groups.
===Factors Affecting Purchasing Power===
Purchasing power is affected by a variety of factors. Inflation is one of the most important factors, as it affects the amount of goods and services that can be purchased with a given amount of money. Inflation is caused by an increase [[In The Money|in the money]] supply, which leads to an increase in prices. Exchange rates also affect purchasing power, as they determine the amount of goods and services that can be purchased with a given currency. The availability of goods and services also affects purchasing power, as it determines the amount of goods and services that can be purchased with a given amount of money.
===Measuring Purchasing Power===
Purchasing power can be measured in a variety of ways. One of the most common methods is to use the [[Consumer Price Index|Consumer Price Index]] (CPI). The CPI measures the average price of a basket of goods and services over time. It is used to measure the purchasing power of a given currency. Other methods of measuring purchasing power include the Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) and the Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER).
===Historical Examples of Purchasing Power===
Throughout history, purchasing power has been affected by a variety of factors. During the Great Depression, for example, purchasing power was greatly reduced due to high levels of unemployment and deflation. During World War , purchasing power was affected by rationing and price controls. In the post-war period, purchasing power was affected by the Bretton Woods system, which established fixed exchange rates between major currencies.
