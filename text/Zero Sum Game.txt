===Introduction to Zero-Sum Game Theory === 
Zero-sum game theory is a branch of game theory that deals with situations in which one person’s gain is another person’s loss. In a zero-sum game, the total gains and losses of all players sum to zero. This means that if one player wins, the other player must lose an equal amount. Zero-sum games are often used to model situations in which two or more parties are in competition with each other.
===Examples of Zero-Sum Games === 
Zero-sum games can be found in many different contexts. Some examples include rock-paper-scissors, poker, and chess. In each of these games, the total gains and losses of all players sum to zero.
===Rock-Paper-Scissors === 
Rock-paper-scissors is a classic example of a zero-sum game. In this game, two players each choose one of three possible moves: rock, paper, or scissors. If both players choose the same move, the game is a draw and no one wins. If one player chooses rock and the other chooses paper, the player who chose paper wins. If one player chooses scissors and the other chooses paper, the player who chose scissors wins. In each case, one player’s gain is the other player’s loss.
===Poker === 
Poker is another example of a zero-sum game. In poker, two or more players compete to win chips by making the best hand. The total amount of chips in the pot is fixed, so if one player wins, the other players must lose an equal amount.
===Chess === 
Chess is yet another example of a zero-sum game. In chess, two players compete to checkmate the other player’s king. If one player checkmates the other player’s king, the game is over and that player wins. The other player must then lose an equal amount.

Finally, they can be used to teach important lessons about strategy and decision-making.

Second, they can be difficult to model accurately, as real-world situations are often more complex than zero-sum games. Finally, they can be difficult to apply to real-world situations, as the strategies used in zero-sum games may not be applicable in the real world.
