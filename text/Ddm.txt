===Introduction to DDM===

The Dividend Discount Model (DDM) is a financial valuation model used to estimate the [[Intrinsic Value|intrinsic value]] of a stock. It is based on the premise that the value of a stock is equal to the [[Present Value|present value]] of all future dividend payments. The DDM is a popular tool among investors and analysts for valuing stocks, as it takes into account the expected future [[Cash Flow|cash flow]]s of a company and the [[Time Value Of Money|time value of money]].
===History of DDM===

The DDM was first developed by John Burr Williams in 1938. Williams was a professor of finance at Harvard University and is considered to be the father of modern finance. He developed the DDM as a way to value stocks based on their expected future dividend payments. Since then, the DDM has been widely used by investors and analysts to value stocks.
===Types of DDM===

There are two main types of DDM: the [[Gordon Growth Model|Gordon Growth Model]] and the Two-Stage Dividend Discount Model. The Gordon Growth Model assumes that dividends will grow at a constant rate in perpetuity. The Two-Stage Dividend Discount Model assumes that dividends will grow at a constant rate for a certain period of time, and then will grow at a different rate after that period.

===Applications of DDM===

The DDM is used by investors and analysts to value stocks. It can be used to compare the intrinsic value of a stock to its current market price, which can help investors make informed decisions about whether to buy or sell a stock.
