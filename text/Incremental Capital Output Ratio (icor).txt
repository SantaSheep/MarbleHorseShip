===Introduction to Incremental Capital Output Ratio (ICOR) === 
Incremental Capital Output Ratio (ICOR) is a measure of the amount of capital required to produce one unit of output. It is used to measure the efficiency of an economy in terms of its ability to generate economic growth. It is a useful tool for economists and policy makers to assess the effectiveness of economic policies and investments.
===Definition of ICOR === 
Incremental Capital Output Ratio (ICOR) is defined as the ratio of the amount of capital required to produce one unit of output. It is calculated by dividing the amount of capital invested in a given period by the amount of output produced in the same period. The higher the ICOR, the less efficient the economy is in terms of generating economic growth.
===Factors Affecting ICOR === 
There are several factors that can affect the ICOR of an economy. These include the level of investment, the rate of technological progress, the availability of resources, the level of government spending, and the level of economic activity.
===Examples of ICOR === 
One example of ICOR is the ratio of the amount of capital invested in a given period to the amount of output produced in the same period. For example, if an economy invests $100 million in a given period and produces $200 million in output in the same period, then the ICOR would be 0.5.
===Benefits of ICOR === 
The ICOR is a useful tool for economists and policy makers to assess the effectiveness of economic policies and investments. It can help to identify areas where investment is needed and can be used to compare the efficiency of different economies. It can also be used to measure the impact of changes in economic policies on economic growth.
