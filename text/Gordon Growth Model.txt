===Introduction to the Gordon Growth Model===
The Gordon Growth Model, also known as the dividend discount model, is a financial model used to value a company’s stock price. It is based on the idea that the value of a stock is equal to the [[Present Value|present value]] of all future dividend payments. The model was developed by Myron Gordon in the 1950s and has since become a widely used tool for valuing stocks.
===History of the Gordon Growth Model===
The Gordon Growth Model was developed by Myron Gordon in the 1950s. Gordon was a professor of finance at the University of Toronto and was interested in finding a way to value stocks. He developed the model as a way to estimate the [[Intrinsic Value|intrinsic value]] of a stock based on its expected future dividend payments. The model has since become a widely used tool for valuing stocks.
===Components of the Gordon Growth Model===
The Gordon Growth Model is based on the idea that the value of a stock is equal to the present value of all future dividend payments. The model takes into account the expected dividend payments, the required [[Rate Of Return|rate of return]], and the growth rate of the dividend payments. The required rate of return is the minimum rate of return that an investor requires in order to invest in a stock. The growth rate of the dividend payments is the rate at which the dividend payments are expected to grow over time.
===Applications of the Gordon Growth Model===
The Gordon Growth Model is used to value stocks and to estimate the intrinsic value of a stock. It is also used to compare the value of a stock to its current market price. If the intrinsic value of a stock is higher than its current market price, then the stock is considered to be undervalued. Conversely, if the intrinsic value of a stock is lower than its current market price, then the stock is considered to be overvalued.
===Limitations of the Gordon Growth Model===
The Gordon Growth Model is a useful tool for valuing stocks, but it has some limitations. The model assumes that the dividend payments will grow at a constant rate, which may not always be the case. Additionally, the model does not take into account other factors that may affect the value of a stock, such as changes in the economy or changes in the company’s business.
