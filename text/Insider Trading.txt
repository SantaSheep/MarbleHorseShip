===Introduction to Insider Trading===

Insider trading is a term used to describe the trading of a company’s securities by individuals who have access to non-public information about the company. This type of trading is illegal in most countries, as it gives the trader an unfair advantage over other investors who do not have access to the same information. Insider trading can be done by corporate insiders, such as directors, officers, and employees, as well as by outsiders who have access to confidential information.
===Definition of Insider Trading===

Insider trading is defined as the buying or selling of a security by someone who has access to material, non-public information about the security. This type of trading is illegal because it gives the trader an unfair advantage over other investors who do not have access to the same information. Insider trading can be done by corporate insiders, such as directors, officers, and employees, as well as by outsiders who have access to confidential information.
===Types of Insider Trading===

There are two types of insider trading: legal and illegal. Legal insider trading is when corporate insiders, such as directors, officers, and employees, trade in the company’s securities based on material, non-public information. This type of trading is legal as long as the insider does not use the information to gain an unfair advantage over other investors. Illegal insider trading is when outsiders, such as family members, friends, or business associates, trade in the company’s securities based on material, non-public information. This type of trading is illegal and can result in criminal and civil penalties.
===Legal Implications of Insider Trading===

Insider trading is illegal in most countries and can result in criminal and civil penalties. In the United States, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) has the authority to investigate and prosecute insider trading cases. The SEC can impose civil penalties, such as fines and disgorgement of profits, as well as criminal penalties, such as jail time. In addition, the SEC can bar individuals from serving as officers or directors of public companies.
