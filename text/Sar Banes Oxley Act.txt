===Introduction to the Sarbanes-Oxley Act===

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (SOX) is a federal law that was enacted in response to the corporate scandals of the early 2000s. The law was designed to protect investors from fraudulen[[T Account|t account]]ing practices and to restore public confidence in the [[Financial Market|financial market]]s. The Act is named after its primary sponsors, Senator Paul Sarbanes and Representative Michael Oxley. It is also commonly referred to as the Public Company Accounting Reform and Investor Protection Act.
===Overview of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act===

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act was passed in 2002 in response to the corporate scandals of the early 2000s. The Act was designed to protect investors from fraudulent accounting practices and to restore public confidence in the financial markets. The Act requires public companies to adhere to certain standards of [[Corporate Governance|corporate governance]] and financial reporting. It also established the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) to oversee the auditing of public companies.
===Key Provisions of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act===

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act contains several key provisions that are designed to protect investors and restore public confidence in the financial markets. These provisions include:

• Establishing the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) to oversee the auditing of public companies.

• Requiring public companies to adhere to certain standards of corporate governance and financial reporting.

• Establishing criminal penalties for certain violations of the Act.

• Requiring public companies to disclose any material changes in their financial condition.

• Establishing a [[Code Of Ethics|code of ethics]] for senior financial officers.

• Requiring public companies to maintain accurate books and records.
===Impact of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act===

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act has had a significant impact on the financial markets. The Act has increased the transparency of public companies and has made it more difficult for companies to engage in fraudulent accounting practices. The Act has also increased the cost of doing business for public companies, as they must now comply with the Act’s requirements.
