===Introduction to WACC===

[[Weighted Average|Weighted Average]] [[Cost Of Capital|Cost of Capital]] (WACC) is a financial metric used to measure the cost of capital for a company. It is a weighted average of the [[Cost Of Equity|cost of equity]] and the [[Cost Of Debt|cost of debt]], and is used to determine the cost of financing for a company. WACC is an important metric for investors and analysts to consider when evaluating a company’s [[Financial Performance|financial performance]] and potential for future growth.
===Components of WACC===

The components of WACC are the cost of equity and the cost of debt. The cost of equity is the return that investors expect to receive from investing in a company’s stock. This return is typically determined by the company’s risk profile and the [[Expected Return|expected return]] of the [[Stock Market|stock market]]. The cost of debt is the [[Interest Rate|interest rate]] that a company pays on its debt. This rate is typically determined by the company’s [[Credit Rating|credit rating]] and the current interest rate environment.
===Calculating WACC===

WACC is calculated by taking the weighted average of the cost of equity and the cost of debt. The weighting is determined by the proportion of equity and debt that a company has in its [[Capital Structure|capital structure]]. For example, if a company has $100 million in equity and $50 million in debt, the weighting would be 2:1 (equity:debt). The cost of equity and the cost of debt are then multiplied by their respective weights and added together to calculate the WACC.
===Advantages and Disadvantages of WACC===

The main advantage of WACC is that it provides a comprehensive measure of a company’s cost of capital. By taking into account both the cost of equity and the cost of debt, WACC provides a more accurate picture of a company’s cost of financing than either measure alone. Additionally, WACC is relatively easy to calculate and can be used to compare the cost of capital of different companies.

The main disadvantage of WACC is that it does not take into account the risk associated with different sources of capital. For example, debt is typically more risky than equity, but WACC does no[[T Account|t account]] for this risk. Additionally, WACC does not take into account the tax implications of different sources of capital, which can have a significant impact on a company’s cost of capital.
