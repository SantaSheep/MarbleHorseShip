===Introduction to Accredited Investor===

Accredited investor is a term used to describe a person or entity that meets certain criteria set by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). Accredited investors are typically high [[Net Worth|net worth]] individuals or entities that have the financial sophistication and capacity to take on the risks associated with investing in certain types of securities. Accredited investors are allowed to invest in certain types of securities that are not available to the general public.
===Definition of Accredited Investor===

The SEC defines an accredited investor as a person or entity that meets certain criteria. These criteria include having a net worth of at least $1 million, excluding the value of their primary residence, or having an income of at least $200,000 in each of the last two years and a reasonable expectation of the same income level in the current year. Entities such as banks, insurance companies, registered investment advisors, and business development companies are also considered accredited investors.
===Qualifications of an Accredited Investor===

In order to qualify as an accredited investor, an individual must meet certain criteria set by the SEC. These criteria include having a net worth of at least $1 million, excluding the value of their primary residence, or having an income of at least $200,000 in each of the last two years and a reasonable expectation of the same income level in the current year. Entities such as banks, insurance companies, registered investment advisors, and business development companies are also considered accredited investors.
===Benefits of Being an Accredited Investor===

Accredited investors have access to certain types of investments that are not available to the general public. These investments include private placements, [[Hedge Fund|hedge fund]]s, [[Venture Capital|venture capital]] funds, and other types of investments that are not registered with the SEC. Accredited investors also have access to more information about the investments they are considering, which can help them make more informed decisions.
===Risks of Being an Accredited Investor===

Although there are many benefits to being an accredited investor, there are also risks associated with investing in certain types of securities. These risks include the potential for fraud, the lack of liquidity, and the potential for losses due to market volatility. It is important for accredited investors to understand the risks associated with their investments and to make sure they are comfortable with the level of risk they are taking on.
