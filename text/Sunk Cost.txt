===Introduction to Sunk Cost===

Sunk cost is a term used in economics and finance to refer to costs that have already been incurred and cannot be recovered. It is a concept that is used to explain why people may continue to invest in a project or activity even when it is no longer profitable. Sunk cost is an important concept to understand when making decisions about investments and other financial matters.
===Definition of Sunk Cost===

Sunk cost is defined as a cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered. It is a cost that has already been paid and cannot be changed or reversed. Sunk costs are not relevant to future decisions because they cannot be changed or recovered.
===Examples of Sunk Cost===

A common example of a sunk cost is the cost of purchasing a car. Once the car has been purchased, the cost of the car cannot be recovered. Other examples of sunk costs include the cost of research and development, the cost of advertising, and the cost of training employees.
===Impact of Sunk Cost on Decision Making===

Sunk cost can have a significant impact on decision making. People may be tempted to continue investing in a project or activity even when it is no longer profitable because they have already invested a significant amount of money in it. This is known as the sunk cost fallacy, which is the tendency to continue investing in a project or activity even when it is no longer profitable.
===Strategies for Avoiding Sunk Cost===

There are several strategies that can be used to avoid sunk cost. One strategy is to focus on the future rather than the past. This means that decisions should be based on the potential future returns rather than the past costs. Another strategy is to consider the [[Opportunity Cost|opportunity cost]] of continuing to invest in a project or activity. This means that the potential returns from other investments should be considered when making decisions.
===Conclusion===

Sunk cost is an important concept to understand when making decisions about investments and other financial matters. Sunk cost can have a significant impact on decision making, as people may be tempted to continue investing in a project or activity even when it is no longer profitable. There are several strategies that can be used to avoid sunk cost, such as focusing on the future rather than the past and considering the opportunity cost of continuing to invest in a project or activity
