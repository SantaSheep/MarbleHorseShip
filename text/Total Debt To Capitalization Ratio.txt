===Introduction to Total Debt-to-Capitalization Ratio===

Total Debt-to-Capitalization Ratio (/) is a financial ratio that measures the amount of debt a company has relative to its total capitalization. It is used to assess the financial health of a company and to compare it to other companies in the same industry. The ratio is calculated by dividing the total debt of a company by its total capitalization, which includes both debt and equity. The higher the ratio, the more debt a company has relative to its total capitalization.
===Calculation of Total Debt-to-Capitalization Ratio===

The Total Debt-to-Capitalization Ratio is calculated by dividing the total debt of a company by its total capitalization. Total debt includes all short-term and long-term debt, such as bonds, loans, and other liabilities. Total capitalization includes both debt and equity, such as common stock, [[Preferred Stock|preferred stock]], and [[Retained Earnings|retained earnings]]. The formula for calculating the ratio is as follows:

Total Debt-to-Capitalization Ratio = Total Debt / Total Capitalization
===Significance of Total Debt-to-Capitalization Ratio===

The Total Debt-to-Capitalization Ratio is an important financial ratio that can be used to assess the financial health of a company. A high ratio indicates that a company has a large amount of debt relative to its total capitalization, which can be a sign of financial distress. On the other hand, a low ratio indicates that a company has a low amount of debt relative to its total capitalization, which can be a sign of financial strength.

The ratio can also be used to compare a company to other companies in the same industry. A higher ratio than the industry average may indicate that a company is taking on more risk than its peers, while a lower ratio than the industry average may indicate that a company is taking on less risk than its peers.
