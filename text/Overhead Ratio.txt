===Introduction to Overhead Ratio===
The overhead ratio is a financial metric used to measure the efficiency of a company’s operations. It is calculated by dividing the total overhead costs of a company by its total revenue. The overhead ratio is an important tool for businesses to measure their operational efficiency and to identify areas where they can improve their operations. It is also used by investors and analysts to assess the financial health of a company.

===Components of Overhead Ratio===
The overhead ratio is composed of two components: overhead costs and total revenue. Overhead costs include all costs associated with running a business that are not directly related to the production of goods or services. These costs include rent, utilities, insurance, taxes, and other administrative costs. Total revenue is the total amount of money a company earns from the sale of goods or services.
===Calculating Overhead Ratio===
The overhead ratio is calculated by dividing the total overhead costs of a company by its total revenue. For example, if a company has total overhead costs of $100,000 and total revenue of $500,000, the overhead ratio would be 0.2 or 20%. This means that for every dollar of revenue, the company spends 20 cents on overhead costs.

===Limitations of Overhead Ratio===
The overhead ratio is a useful tool for measuring operational efficiency, but it has some limitations. The overhead ratio does not take into account the quality of the goods or services produced by a company, or the [[Cost Of Labor|cost of labor]]. Additionally, the overhead ratio does not take into account the company’s long-term financial goals or its ability to generate profits.
