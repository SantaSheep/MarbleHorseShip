===Introduction to Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) === 
Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) is a financial ratio that measures the relative proportion of a company’s debt and equity. It is used to assess a company’s financial leverage and is an important indicator of a company’s financial health. The ratio is calculated by dividing a company’s total liabilities by its total equity. A higher ratio indicates that a company is more leveraged and has a higher risk of defaulting on its debt.
===What is Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E)? === 
The Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) is a financial ratio that measures the relative proportion of a company’s debt and equity. It is calculated by dividing a company’s total liabilities by its total equity. A higher ratio indicates that a company is more leveraged and has a higher risk of defaulting on its debt.
===How to Calculate Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) === 
The Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) is calculated by dividing a company’s total liabilities by its total equity. Total liabilities include all short-term and long-term debt, such as loans, bonds, and other financial obligations. Total equity includes all shareholders’ equity, such as common stock, retained earnings, and other equity accounts.
===Benefits of Knowing Your Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) === 
Knowing your Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) can be beneficial for a number of reasons. It can help you assess the financial health of a company and determine whether it is over-leveraged or under-leveraged. It can also help you determine the risk of defaulting on debt and the ability of a company to pay its debts.
===Examples of Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E)=== 
A company with a Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) of 1.0 has equal amounts of debt and equity. A company with a Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) of 2.0 has twice as much debt as equity. A company with a Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E) of 0.5 has half as much debt as equity.
